## Executive Summary

Artist collective governance falls into three primary models: **Board-Governed** (traditional nonprofit), **Worker Cooperative** (democratic member control), and **Collective Leadership** (distributed power). Each has distinct tradeoffs for decision-making speed, member autonomy, and founder dependency.

**Critical Finding**: Most arts organizations use board governance, but this model is "setting us up to fail" according to Australian arts leaders (ArtsHub, 2026) because boards lack operational expertise and slow decision-making. Worker cooperative models give artists more control but require consensus-building that can paralyze action.

---

## Model 1: Board-Governed (Traditional Nonprofit)

**Structure**: Board of directors provides oversight, executive director handles operations, artists are beneficiaries/participants (not decision-makers).

**Decision-Making**: Board sets strategy, ED executes, artists have advisory role.

**Advantages**:
- Clear accountability
- Meets 501(c)(3) requirements
- Attracts experienced board members
- Separates governance from operations

**Disadvantages**:
- "Arts boards are no strangers to spreadsheets and financial data, but effective governance requires a much wider set of experience and skills" (ArtsHub, 2026)
- Board members often lack operational arts expertise
- Slow decision-making (board meets quarterly)
- Artists have limited voice

**Founder Dependency Risk**: High - if founder is ED, departure creates leadership vacuum.

---

## Model 2: Worker Cooperative

**Structure**: "Cooperative owned and self-managed by its workers... every worker-owner participates in decision-making in a democratic fashion" (Wikipedia).

**Decision-Making**: One member, one vote. Major decisions require member approval.

**Advantages**:
- Artists have direct control
- Democratic legitimacy
- Aligns incentives (members benefit from success)
- Low founder dependency (distributed ownership)

**Disadvantages**:
- Slow decision-making (consensus required)
- Difficult to scale (more members = harder consensus)
- May not qualify for 501(c)(3) (member benefit issue)
- Requires high member engagement

**Founder Dependency Risk**: Low - ownership is distributed.

---

## Model 3: Collective Leadership

**Structure**: "Shared distribution of power within an organizational structure... supposed to be only primus inter pares [first among equals]" (Wikipedia).

**Decision-Making**: Leadership team makes decisions collectively, no single authority.

**Advantages**:
- Faster than full democracy
- Distributes expertise (different leaders for different domains)
- Reduces founder dependency
- Flexible (can adapt structure as needed)

**Disadvantages**:
- Requires high trust among leaders
- Conflict resolution is difficult (no tiebreaker)
- May lack clear accountability
- Can devolve into "design by committee"

**Founder Dependency Risk**: Medium - depends on how distributed leadership actually is.

---

## Model 4: Hybrid (Board + Artist Advisory Council)

**Structure**: Board provides governance, artist advisory council has formal input on artistic decisions.

**Decision-Making**: Board handles fiduciary/legal, artists handle curatorial/artistic.

**Advantages**:
- Meets 501(c)(3) requirements (board governance)
- Artists have voice (advisory council)
- Clear separation of concerns
- Balances accountability and participation

**Disadvantages**:
- Two bodies to coordinate
- Potential for conflict (board vs artists)
- Advisory council may feel powerless if board overrides

**Founder Dependency Risk**: Medium - depends on board composition and succession planning.

## Key Insights for Rossi Mission Project

### Insight 1: Board Governance Alone is Insufficient

"Arts boards... effective governance requires a much wider set of experience and skills" beyond financial oversight. **Implication**: If Rossi uses board model, board must include artists, curators, and community members, not just business/finance people.

### Insight 2: Worker Cooperative Conflicts with 501(c)(3)

Member-owned cooperatives may not qualify for 501(c)(3) due to "private benefit" issue. **Implication**: If Rossi wants 501(c)(3), worker cooperative is not viable.

### Insight 3: Hybrid Model Balances Tradeoffs

Board + Artist Advisory Council gives artists voice while meeting 501(c)(3) requirements. **Implication**: This is likely the optimal governance model for Rossi.

### Insight 4: Decentralized Decision-Making Requires Infrastructure

"Decentralized decision-making... connotes a higher authority given to lower level functionaries" (Wikipedia) - but this requires clear decision rights and escalation paths. **Implication**: If Rossi distributes decision-making, it must document who decides what.

## Recommended Governance Structure for Rossi

**Tier 1: Board of Directors** (5-7 members)
- Fiduciary oversight
- Strategic direction
- Legal/financial accountability
- Composition: 2 artists, 2 business/finance, 1 legal, 1 community, 1 founder (non-voting advisor after transition)

**Tier 2: Artist Advisory Council** (10-15 members)
- Curatorial decisions
- Artist selection/promotion
- Exhibition planning
- Community engagement
- Elected by artist community (1-year terms)

**Tier 3: Executive Director**
- Day-to-day operations
- Staff management
- Fundraising
- Partnership development

**Decision Rights**:
- Board: Budget, major contracts, ED hiring/firing, strategic plan
- Artist Council: Exhibition calendar, artist selection, curatorial standards
- ED: Operations, staff, vendor contracts, day-to-day decisions
